# Target Audience: Career Switchers

## Profile

People transitioning into tech from unrelated fields -- the fastest-growing
developer segment and the most underserved by existing platforms.

**Who they are:**
- Professionals from non-tech industries (finance, healthcare, education, marketing)
- Bootcamp graduates pivoting into development
- Self-taught learners who completed online courses but lack real-world experience
- People in tech-adjacent roles (QA, project management, design) moving into engineering

**Scale:**
- **1 in 3** tech workers entered from an unrelated field
- **81%** of employers now prefer skills over formal degrees (up from 56% in 2022)
- Career changers are the **#1 mentee segment** (30% of all mentorship seekers)

## Key Challenges

### 1. The Credibility Gap
- Employers see a non-CS resume and immediately discount the candidate
- Bootcamp certificates are increasingly viewed with skepticism
- Portfolio projects (to-do apps, clones) don't differentiate from other switchers
- No professional references who can speak to coding ability

### 2. The Experience Chicken-and-Egg
- Same paradox as juniors, but worse -- no internship pipeline, no campus recruiting
- Average time to first tech job for career changers: **6-12 months**
- Competing against CS graduates AND experienced developers for the same "entry-level" roles
- Often have financial obligations (mortgages, families) that make unpaid learning unsustainable

### 3. Skills vs. Credentials
- Completed 10 courses on Udemy but can't prove production-level ability
- Online course completion rates: **5-15%** (even those who finish lack real-world skills)
- **87%** of developers prefer learning by doing over learning by watching
- "Tutorial hell" is a documented phenomenon -- completing tutorials without being able to build independently

### 4. The Mentorship Desert
- **76%** say mentors are important for career development
- **54%** lack access to any mentor
- Workers with mentors are **2x more likely** to stay 5+ years in their role
- Existing mentorship platforms (MentorCruise, ADPList) don't involve real work

## What They Want

1. **Real project experience** to put on their resume -- not another certificate
2. **Mentorship** from actual working professionals
3. **A bridge** between "I know how to code" and "I'm a professional developer"
4. **Verified outcomes** that prove ability to employers
5. **Structured learning** in a production environment (not more tutorials)

## How DevStage Solves It

- **Apply to company listings** that match the stack they're learning
- **Work on real projects** with real teams -- the exact experience they lack
- **Outcome criteria** provide structure (clear goals, measurable results)
- **Verified reviews** from companies create credibility that certificates can't
- **Paid engagements** -- sustainable unlike unpaid internships or volunteer open source

## Messaging Angles

- **Primary**: "You learned to code. Now prove it with real work."
- **Transformation**: "From tutorial hell to production reality."
- **Social proof**: "1 in 3 tech workers started somewhere else. Your next chapter starts here."
- **Practical**: "Courses teach syntax. DevStage teaches how to ship."
