# Pain Point: AI-Assisted Cheating in Technical Interviews

## Summary

AI-assisted cheating in coding interviews more than doubled in 2025, with nearly half
of candidates using unauthorized tools. This renders traditional technical interviews
unreliable as a skill assessment method.

## Scale of the Problem

- **48%** of candidates openly admit to using unauthorized AI tools during interviews
- Cheating adoption **more than doubled**: 15% (June 2025) to 35% (December 2025)
  in Fabric's analysis of 50,000+ candidates
- **81%** of interviewers at Big Tech companies have suspected candidates of using AI to cheat
- **31%** have definitively caught a candidate
- One tech leader estimated **80%** of candidates use LLMs on top-of-funnel code tests

> Sources: FabricHQ State of Cheating 2026; CNBC/Google; InterviewQuery 2025

## The Tools

### Interview Coder and Similar Products
- Claimed **65% success rate** helping engineers cheat
- Reported hires at Amazon and Microsoft
- Uses **invisible screen overlays** -- AI-generated answers visible only to the candidate
  while remaining invisible during screen sharing
- **Economics favor cheaters**: $20-50/month subscription vs. $150,000 engineering salary

### How It Works
1. Candidate enters interview (Zoom/Google Meet/Teams)
2. AI tool reads the coding problem from the screen
3. Solution appears in an invisible overlay
4. Candidate types the solution as if working it out
5. Screen sharing shows nothing -- overlay is invisible to interviewers

## Company Responses

- In-person interview rounds rose from **24% in 2022 to 38% in 2025**
- **Google** launched "Project Campfire" with secure, browser-based interview environments
- **Meta** requires interviewers to flag suspected cheating across nearly all interview types
- Companies investing in proctoring software and identity verification

## Why This Is Getting Worse

1. **AI models improve** -- solutions become more accurate and harder to detect
2. **Tools commoditize** -- cheating becomes cheaper and more accessible
3. **Remote work persists** -- video interviews remain standard
4. **Stakes are high** -- $150K salary incentivizes cheating
5. **Arms race** -- every countermeasure spawns a new evasion technique

## Why DevStage Eliminates This Problem

Real project work over weeks/months cannot be cheated the way a 45-minute interview can:

| Interview Cheating | Why It Can't Work on DevStage |
|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| AI-generated code answers | Real projects require sustained understanding, not one-shot answers |
| Invisible screen overlays | Work happens asynchronously on actual codebases |
| Proxy/impersonation | Extended engagement with a team over weeks reveals identity |
| Memorized solutions | Real production problems have no pre-made solutions |

The fundamental shift: **instead of testing what someone knows in a snapshot,
evaluate what they can build over time**.
